About Me

Hello! I'm David Graff, a joint Ph.D. student in the Coley group at MIT ChemE and Shakhnovich group at Harvard CCB. In my studies, I focus on developing algorithms and software to accelerate computational drug discovery at ultra-large scales. I'm particularly interested in merging novel techniques in optimization with massive computing problems and thinking about new ways in which computers can aid in the discovery of novel chemistries.

I grew up in Fort Lauderdale, FL and completed my undergraduate studies at Princeton University. It was there that I was first exposed to both organic chemistry and computer science. My formative research experiences were as a member of the Knowles group working in reaction development and that eventually led me to the Ph.D. program at Harvard.

After a little (some might say "a lot") soul-searching, I joined the Coley and Shanovich groups as a collaborative student. Our collaboration focuses on identifying new antibiotics in compound libraries containing billions of molecules while simultaneously limiting the total computational cost. My research cuts across many domains of science: computational chemistry, drug discovery, optimization, and scientific computing.

Outside of research, I like to spend a lot of my time outdoors running, cycling, or hiking. Between fall and spring, I can usually be found somwhere on the Charles River training for a marathon or other road race, and in the summer I'm either out on my bike for too long or waking up too early to go for a hike. I also enjoy cooking and baking, so much so my friends and I used to operate a restaurant out of a dorm room in undergrad. However, these days, I stick to slightly less stressful pursuits.
